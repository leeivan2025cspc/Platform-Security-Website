Lesson 2: Understanding the Risk Management Process

Information Security Risk

Information security risk refers to the potential adverse impacts that result from the possibility of unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction of information and/or information systems.

Risk Management Terminology

  • Asset: Anything of value that needs protection — tangible or intangible.
  • Vulnerability: A gap or weakness in protection efforts that can be exploited.
  • Threat: Something or someone that attempts to exploit a vulnerability.

Example:

Tourists are popular targets for pickpockets. The existence of pickpockets in a crowded tourist spot is a threat. If a pickpocket targets you, they become the threat actor, and their method is the threat vector.

Threat Actors

  • Insiders: Employees or personnel who may cause harm deliberately or by mistake.
  • Outside Individuals/Groups: Hackers or opportunists exploiting vulnerabilities.
  • Non-Political Entities: Cybercriminals or competitors.
  • Political Entities: Nation-states, terrorists, or hacktivists.
  • Information Gatherers: Any actor seeking intelligence or sensitive data.
  • Technology: Automated tools, bots, or AI used in cyberattacks.

Vulnerabilities

A vulnerability is an inherent weakness or flaw in a system or component that, if triggered or exploited, could cause a risk event to occur.

Risk Assessment

Risk assessment is the process of identifying, estimating, and prioritizing risks to an organization’s operations — including its mission, reputation, assets, and individuals.

Risk Treatment

Risk treatment involves deciding how to respond to identified and prioritized risks. The approach depends on management’s attitude toward risk, resource availability, and mitigation cost.

Main Risk Treatment Options:

  • Avoidance: Eliminating the risk entirely by removing the cause or activity.
  • Acceptance: Choosing to take no action and tolerating the risk.
  • Mitigation: Reducing the likelihood or impact of the risk through proactive measures.
  • Transference: Shifting the risk to another party (e.g., through insurance or outsourcing).
